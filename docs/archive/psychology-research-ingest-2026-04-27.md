## Source-by-source extraction

### 1. Loewenstein 1996 — "Out of Control: Visceral Influences on Behavior"

**The redteam question this answers:** Is "energy" (Low/Medium/High self-report) a meaningful filter, or does visceral-state research suggest hot/cold cognitive states matter more than slow self-categorization?

**What the paper actually says:** Loewenstein argues that visceral factors — drive states, moods, emotions, fatigue, craving — have a *direct hedonic impact* and *change the relative desirability of goods and actions* in ways that cannot be reduced to "tastes" (p. 273). His central claims: (1) immediately experienced visceral factors disproportionately drive behavior and "crowd out virtually all goals other than that of mitigating the visceral factor"; (2) people *underweight* visceral factors they will experience in the future, have experienced in the past, or that are experienced by others (p. 272). Visceral factors change *much more rapidly* than tastes (p. 273), and at sufficient intensity they "virtually preclude decision making" (p. 275). Critically, behavior driven by visceral factors can occur "without conscious cognitive mediation" (p. 275). Proposition 2 (p. 277) says future visceral factors produce only minimal discrepancy between *planned* value and *experienced* value — i.e., people are bad at predicting their own future hot states from a current cold state.

**Implication for our "energy" picker input:** This is a *partial contradiction* of the energy tier. Loewenstein's research supports the idea that current state matters more than abstract scheduling — so "how are you feeling right now" is more predictive of what you'll actually play than "how long do you have." That's a point in energy's favor versus time. **But** the same research undermines the *self-report* mechanic: visceral states bypass deliberation and are systematically misjudged by the person experiencing them, especially across the very short delay between answering the picker and starting the game. Asking a user to self-categorize as Low/Medium/High energy turns a visceral signal into a cognitive one — exactly the translation Loewenstein says people are bad at. The research weakly supports an energy-style input as *concept*, but suggests the implementation should lean on environmental proxies (time of day, session length, last-played) rather than slow self-categorization. If we keep self-reported energy, we're betting on introspection that this paper says is unreliable.

### 2. Mischel & Shoda 1995 — "A Cognitive-Affective System Theory of Personality"

**The redteam question this answers:** Does self-reported state predict behavior reliably across time?

**What the paper actually says:** The paper's central finding is that *cross-situational consistency* in behavior is low (correlations averaging ~0.2–0.3) but *intra-individual situation–behavior profiles* are stable (mean stability r ≈ .47 for verbal aggression, .41 for compliance, p. 249–250). What predicts a person's behavior is not a global trait but their *if X situation, then Y behavior* signature — which is stable over time within a person but doesn't aggregate into a single dispositional score. Critically: people who *perceive themselves as consistent* are not actually more cross-situationally consistent — but their situation–behavior profiles are more stable (p. 251, Fig. 3). The authors argue for "cognitive-affective units" (encodings, expectancies, affects, goals, self-regulatory plans, Table 1) whose chronic activation differs across people, and whose *organization* — not their average level — is what makes a personality.

**Implication for our "energy" picker input and our archetypes:** Two-edged. **Against energy/mood self-report:** asking users to self-categorize global state ("I am low-energy today") is exactly the dispositional aggregate that Mischel & Shoda show *doesn't predict behavior well*. What predicts behavior is the if-then pattern (*if it's after dinner on a weeknight, I want short comfort sessions*). A picker asking "how are you feeling?" treats the user as a trait-bearer when the research says they're a context-bearer. **For archetypes:** the paper validates archetypes more than it threatens them — a "Deep Diver" archetype is essentially an if-then signature ("if a long RPG is in your library, then you start it"). That's exactly the shape Mischel & Shoda say *is* real. But this support depends on the archetype being derived from observed behavior patterns, not self-assigned. We do that — archetypes come from library composition and play behavior — so the construct has empirical legs the rules file isn't currently citing.

### 3. Deci & Ryan 2000 — Self-Determination Theory

**The redteam question this answers:** Does identity-affirming feedback like archetypes increase desired behavior or substitute for it?

**What the paper actually says:** SDT identifies three innate psychological needs: *competence*, *autonomy*, and *relatedness* (p. 68). When satisfied, intrinsic motivation flourishes; when thwarted, motivation collapses. The most consequential finding for us: *tangible extrinsic rewards reliably undermine intrinsic motivation* — the Deci/Koestner/Ryan (1999) meta-analysis "confirmed... that all expected tangible rewards made contingent on task performance do reliably undermine intrinsic motivation" (p. 70). The same effect applies to "threats, deadlines, directives, pressured evaluations, and imposed goals" because they shift the perceived locus of causality from internal to external (p. 70). Conversely, "choice, acknowledgment of feelings, and opportunities for self-direction... enhance intrinsic motivation" (p. 70). Positive performance feedback *enhances* intrinsic motivation only when it carries information about competence without controlling overtones (p. 70, citing Vallerand & Reid 1984).

**Implication for our archetypes (and the celebration/share-card apparatus):** SDT is *cautiously supportive* of archetypes — they're informational identity feedback, not contingent rewards, and they affirm competence ("you're a Deep Diver" = you're someone who finishes meaty things). They're not delivered contingent on a task we asked the user to perform, so they don't trigger the reward-undermining effect. But the line is thin. **Three risks the paper flags by implication:** (1) If archetypes start being *delivered* in response to specific actions ("you completed 3 RPGs, you've unlocked the Deep Diver title"), they become contingent extrinsic rewards and per the meta-analysis will *reduce* the intrinsic motivation to play. (2) If archetypes feel *evaluative* ("here's how you rank") rather than *informational* ("here's a pattern in what you love"), they shift locus of causality outward. (3) If sharing becomes about display rather than expression, it can undermine the autonomy support that makes the rest of the product work. The current archetype implementation seems on the right side of all three lines, but the research says the failure mode is real and one product change away.

### 4. Berger 2013 — *Contagious* / STEPPS framework

**The redteam question this answers:** Does our share card hit Practical Value or Social Currency? Which survives, which decays?

**What the paper actually says:** Berger's STEPPS framework names six drivers of word-of-mouth: **S**ocial Currency, **T**riggers, **E**motion, **P**ublic, **P**ractical Value, **S**tories (Introduction; chapter list). Social Currency is "we share things that make us look good" (Ch. 1). Practical Value is "news you can use" — useful information shared because it helps the recipient (Ch. 5, "E-mail forwards are the new barn raising"). Berger doesn't explicitly rank decay rates of these drivers in the chapter list excerpts available, but the framing makes clear that Social Currency is *self-presentation* (sender-benefit) while Practical Value is *help* (recipient-benefit). Triggers ("top of mind, tip of tongue") and Public ("built to show, built to grow") are the structural drivers that determine repeat exposure independent of the message's content.

**Implication for our share cards:** Our archetype/clear/pile share cards are pure *Social Currency* — they make the sharer look like a discerning, self-aware gamer. They are not Practical Value to the recipient (they don't help the recipient pick a game; the recipient isn't even our user yet). The redteam's worry is correct: Social Currency drives initial bursts but decays once the novelty of the format wears off and the sharer no longer looks novel for posting one. Practical Value compounds because the value to the recipient doesn't decay. **The honest reading:** our share apparatus is built on the decaying driver. To get durable virality, the card or accompanying flow would need to do something useful for the *recipient* (give them a free pick, surface a deal on a game they own, let them try the picker without sign-up) — i.e., add a Practical Value layer on top of the Social Currency one. Without that, share cards are a launch-spike feature, not a flywheel. (Berger does also stress Triggers and Public, which are partly working in our favor — the card is highly visible/Public and the inventory metaphor is somewhat trigger-rich.)

### 5. Hu et al. 2014 — "What We Instagram"

**The redteam question this answers:** Does the share-creation moment pull users back into the app on a non-pick session, violating less-time-in-app?

**What the paper actually says:** A content analysis of 1,000 Instagram photos from 50 active users yielded eight categories. The most striking finding: **46.6% of all photos were Selfies (24.2%) or Friends (22.4%)** — nearly half of all content posted is self-presentational (p. 597, Fig. 2). Selfies and Friends categories also had the *lowest* standard deviations across users (SD = 0.11 and 0.124), meaning self-presentation is the most universally distributed posting behavior — it's not a niche; it's the floor (p. 597). The clustering analysis (Fig. 4) found a "selfies-lovers" cluster (C4, 6% of users) who post self-portraits almost exclusively. Crucially: the paper also found "the size of a user's audience (followers) is independent of his/her shared photos" (p. 595, 598) — i.e., posting more selfies does not produce more followers. **Followers do not reward self-presentation.** Hu et al. don't directly study return-to-app behavior on non-posting sessions, so they don't answer the redteam's question head-on; what they show is that self-presentation behavior is dominant and *not* externally validated by audience growth.

**Implication for our share cards:** The "share-creators come back to admire their card" failure mode is plausibly real — Hu et al. show that nearly half of social-media posting is self-presentational by content, and the selfies-lover cluster posts *exclusively* in that mode. If a share card is a quasi-selfie of one's gaming identity, we should expect a meaningful subset of users to engage with it the way Instagram users engage with selfies: re-view, re-tweak, return. That violates "less time in app." However, Hu et al.'s finding that audience size is *independent* of self-presentation content means the engagement pattern is intrinsic, not socially reinforced — which weakly suggests the engagement is bounded and self-limiting rather than escalating. **Net read:** the redteam's worry is legitimate but probably second-order. The bigger risk this paper flags is that *we* might be tempted to design share-flow features that mimic Instagram's mechanics (re-edit, re-share, multiple variants), which Hu et al.'s data suggests would absolutely produce return visits. We should not.

### 6. Nielsen — Response Time Limits

**The redteam question this answers:** Is the 60-second axiom right, too aggressive, or too lax?

**What the paper actually says:** Nielsen restates the three limits from Miller (1968) and Card et al. (1991): **0.1 second** = limit for feeling the system reacts instantaneously / direct manipulation; **1.0 second** = limit for keeping the user's flow of thought uninterrupted; **10 seconds** = limit for keeping the user's attention focused on the dialogue. Beyond 10 seconds, "users will want to perform other tasks while waiting." In the 2014 update, Nielsen reaffirms these are user-experience limits independent of implementation: "These guidelines have been the same for 46 years now, so they are also not likely to change with whatever implementation technology comes next." Past 10s, percent-done indicators are required and "users will need to reorient themselves when they return to the UI after a delay of more than 10 seconds." Nielsen does not address a 60-second figure; in his framework, 60s is well past the attention-loss boundary, not at it.

**Implication for our 60-second axiom:** Nielsen's research *contradicts* the framing that 60s is the engagement boundary. **10 seconds is the boundary for sustained attention on a single dialogue.** Once we exceed 10s, the user is mentally task-switching and will need to re-orient when we hand them the pick. The redteam called this exactly right: 60s is the outer envelope including the launcher pivot, not the target for the decision moment itself. The picker's *decision-to-pick* duration should ideally be sub-10s — anything past that and we've lost the user's contiguous attention before the pick is delivered. The "import → pick" total can stretch to 60s because import has a legitimate progress-indicator slot, but the moment from "I clicked Pick My Game" to "here is your game on screen" should be <1s, and the moment from landing the pick to starting the launcher pivot should not require the user to re-engage cognitively. **The current axiom is directionally right but the number is wrong.** It's not aggressive enough.

## Cross-cutting findings

**Where the sources reinforce each other.** Loewenstein and Mischel & Shoda agree, from different angles, that self-reported global state is a poor predictor of action. Loewenstein says it's because visceral factors bypass deliberation and shift faster than introspection can catch; Mischel & Shoda say it's because behavior is organized as if-then situation responses, not as global trait levels. Both findings undermine the "how are you feeling?" picker input as currently conceived. Nielsen reinforces the same theme operationally: by the time a user has cognitively self-categorized their energy, they've burned the attention window where the pick needs to land.

**Where they contradict.** Berger's framework treats Social Currency as a legitimate sharing driver; SDT (Deci & Ryan) treats anything that shifts perceived locus of causality outward as a threat to intrinsic motivation. These collide on share cards. A share card optimized purely for Social Currency (look how cool my pile is) is exactly the kind of external validation device that SDT predicts will shift a user's reason for playing from intrinsic enjoyment to extrinsic display — and Hu et al.'s data on Instagram self-presentation patterns shows the audience reinforcement *isn't even there* to justify the trade. The honest synthesis: share cards hit Berger's growth driver but pay an SDT cost the rules file currently doesn't acknowledge.

**The overall picture.** The product's pick-flow thesis (reduce choice, deliver one game, preserve autonomy) is research-aligned at the macro level. The two underexplored failure modes are: (a) the *energy* input substituting introspection for the visceral signal it's supposed to capture, and (b) the *share apparatus* creating an SDT-inverse — extrinsic-reward-shaped feedback that quietly reframes "playing" as "looking like a player." Neither is fatal. Both deserve an explicit research-grounded position in the rules file rather than the current silence.

## Recommended changes to product

1. **Rebaseline the energy input against Loewenstein + Mischel & Shoda before launch.** The research does not support a self-reported global energy tier as a reliable predictor of what someone wants to play. Either substitute environmental proxies (time of day, last-played duration) or revert to time-tier. The current intervention #1 in the redteam doc ("Rebaseline the picker against the 2-input rule") is now backed by two new sources, not just an internal axiom — it gets stronger, not weaker.

2. **Audit archetype delivery for contingency creep.** Per SDT: any archetype that gets *unlocked*, *earned*, or *announced as a result of a specific tracked action* crosses from informational to contingent extrinsic reward, and the meta-analysis says that reduces play. If we ever consider gamifying archetype reveals, the research says don't.

3. **Add a Practical Value layer to the share-card flow** if we want share cards to have flywheel effects beyond the launch spike. A share card that gives the recipient a free pick, or surfaces a deal on a game they likely own, converts a Social Currency artifact into a Practical Value one. Without this, share cards are a launch tactic, not a growth tactic — Berger's framework predicts the decay.

4. **Tighten the timing target inside the pick flow.** Per Nielsen, the click-to-pick-on-screen response should be <1s, not "<60s end-to-end." The 60s envelope is fine as a marketing/axiom number but the internal engineering target needs to be the 0.1s/1s/10s tiers.

5. **Do not build re-edit / re-share / multi-variant share-card features.** Hu et al.'s Instagram data predicts these mechanics will produce return visits in self-presentational sessions, which is the exact failure mode the "less time in app" axiom is designed to prevent.
