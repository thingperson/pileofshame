# LinkedIn Launch Posts — Inventory Full

## 1. Recommendation

**Post Angle A first.** Brady's LinkedIn audience is brand strategists, founders, and agency people — they don't care about his backlog, but they care a lot about the unfashionable take "I built a tool designed to be used less." That's a positioning argument disguised as a launch, and positioning arguments draw comments from people who want to push back or pile on. Angle B is the safer, more popular post (everyone is talking about AI build velocity), but it's also a saturated take — it gets likes, not replies. Post A first to provoke the conversation, then post B 5–7 days later riding the same audience with a different lens. If A flops in the first 90 minutes, B is the warm backup.

---

## 2. Angle A — The product/psychology angle

> Most product instincts are wrong about backlog tools.
>
> I just shipped one that's designed to make you close it.
>
> 17 years in brand strategy taught me one thing: the category line beats the feature list. So when I built Inventory Full, I started there.
>
> Every other app in this space wants you to catalogue, organize, track, log, score. Engagement metric goes up, problem stays the same — you still own 300 games and still can't decide what to play tonight.
>
> The thesis I built around: a gaming backlog isn't a task list. It's 300 possible selves you haven't committed to. Choice overload, not laziness.
>
> So the app picks one game for you. Mood, time you've got, hit go. That's it. The win-state is you closing the tab.
>
> Less time in the product = success. Every metric a normal PM would optimize, I'm pointed the other way on.
>
> It's at inventoryfull.gg if you want to poke it. Free, no sign-up.
>
> Question for the brand and product people here: where else does "use this less" actually work as a positioning line? I can think of two or three categories. Curious what you'd add.

*(Word count: ~205. Hook lands in two lines. One question at close. Zero hashtags — Brady's audience isn't searching tags, they're scrolling a follow graph.)*

## 3. Angle B — The AI/non-developer angle

> 17 years writing brand strategy. Zero years writing code.
>
> Four days ago I shipped a real web app. Live, used by strangers, sending error reports to Sentry like a grown-up.
>
> I built it with Claude Code. Not "AI helped me." I mean I described what I wanted, in the same language I'd use with a junior designer, and it wrote the thing.
>
> Here's what I actually learned, and it's not the headline you'd expect:
>
> The leverage isn't in the code. The leverage is in knowing what to build and why. I spent maybe 20% of those four days "developing" and 80% making product calls — what to cut, what's on-brand, what serves the user vs. what flatters me.
>
> Translation for the strategists reading this: the moat just moved. It's not technical skill. It's taste, judgment, and being able to say no to your own ideas.
>
> Junior people who can think clearly about a problem just got a massive promotion. Senior people who only know "how" without "why" just got demoted.
>
> The app's at inventoryfull.gg. Built it for myself because my Steam library has 750 games and I can't pick one.
>
> Strategists, founders, agency owners — what's the first thing you'd try to build now that the floor is this low?

*(Word count: ~215. Hook is the 17/0 contrast — punchy, immediate. Stake-in-the-ground claim in the middle. Question that flatters the reader at the end.)*

## 5. Comment-response prep

**"Isn't this just [Backloggd / GameTrack / a spreadsheet]?" — the skeptical agency person**
> Those are trackers. They log what you've played. This decides what you play next — different job, different category. Tracker users still have the paralysis problem at 8pm on a Tuesday.

**"How did you actually use Claude Code? Walk me through it." — the curious peer**
> Mostly conversational. I'd describe the surface I wanted, paste the brand rules, and iterate. Where it shined wasn't writing code — it was holding the spec accountable when I tried to scope-creep myself.

**"Congrats on shipping!! 🎉" — the performative-praise reply**
> Appreciate it. Funny thing is the hard part wasn't shipping — it was deleting features. Probably killed 12 ideas for every one that survived.

**"What's the business model?" — the founder**
> Donationware for now. No ads, no data sharing, no upsell on the core loop — those would all fight the thesis. Eventually probably a small one-time unlock for power features, but I'm letting usage tell me what those are instead of guessing.

**"Honestly I have this exact problem" — the gamer-in-disguise**
> You and roughly everyone I've talked to. The library got bigger than the lifespan years ago and nobody's been honest about it. Try the app — would genuinely like to know if it lands for you.

## 6. Image / visual recommendation

**Attach the OG share card (the archetype-forward one from `app/clear/[id]/`-style render or root OG), NOT a UI screenshot.**

Reasoning: LinkedIn posts with images get reach, but a screenshot of the picker UI does the wrong job — it makes the post look like a SaaS launch and pulls the reader into "evaluate this product" mode. The OG card is brand-forward: wordmark, tagline, one strong visual. It functions as identity, not demo. It sells the *position* (which is what Angle A is doing) rather than the *features* (which would make Angle A's argument weaker).

For Angle B specifically: same call. The OG card. A "look at my code" screenshot reads tryhard for a 17-year strategist audience. The brand asset reads like a person who knows what they're doing finally shipped a thing — which is the actual story.

If Brady has a clean shot of the picker mid-result (mood/length inputs visible + the one-game pick), that's a viable second image in a multi-image post. But the first image stays the OG card. The whole point of Angle A is that this isn't another feature-flexing product post — leading with the brand asset proves that before the reader finishes reading.
